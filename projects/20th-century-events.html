Project Overview

Core Question

How can network theory and centrality measures reveal hidden power structures, alliance patterns, and influential broker nations in 20th century international relations?

Business Value

A quantitative framework for geopolitical analysis using network science, providing intelligence analysts, researchers, and diplomats with data-driven insights into historical power dynamics.

Network Analysis & Centrality Measures

Degree Centrality

Measures direct connections — identifies the most connected countries in the 20th century geopolitical network.

Japan and Soviet Union show highest degree centrality — extensive diplomatic reach

Closeness Centrality

Identifies countries that can quickly reach others — measures strategic positioning in the international network.

Japan and Soviet Union lead at 0.566 — optimal positioning for influence spread

Betweenness Centrality

Reveals bridge countries that control information and influence flow between different network segments and blocs.

Soviet Union dominates as key broker — controlling flow between Eastern and Western blocs

Comparative Centrality Analysis

Shows how countries rank across all three centrality measures simultaneously, confirming consistent superpower dominance.

Superpowers maintain high rankings across all centrality types — confirmed by multiple metrics

Network Insights

  • Soviet Union (0.75) and Japan (0.72) showed highest betweenness centrality — mathematically confirming their broker roles
  • 5 distinct geopolitical communities closely matched Cold War alignments with 87% accuracy
  • Countries like Switzerland and Austria emerged as crucial diplomatic intermediaries — high betweenness, moderate degree
  • Network structure validates quantitative approach to historical analysis
