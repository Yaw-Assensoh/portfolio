Project Overview

Core Question

Which countries are the primary sources and destinations for migrants, and how have these flows evolved over 25 years?

Business Value

An interactive global dashboard visualising migration corridors and quantifying net migration trends — giving NGOs, policymakers, and urban planners evidence-based tools for resource allocation, bilateral agreements, and infrastructure planning.

Continental Migration Analysis

Total net migration across six continents — showing the global scale and direction of population movements over the 25-year period.

Asia leads at 5.8M net migration, followed by North America (3.4M) and Europe (3.2M)

Economic Development vs Migration

Comparison of normalised GDP per capita against migration rates by continent — quantifying the economic pull factor that drives migration decisions.

Europe: highest GDP (100%) with 51.6% migration rate — the wealth-migration correlation confirmed at 0.62

Countries with the highest immigration and emigration rates globally — the specific corridors where bilateral policy, integration programmes, and infrastructure planning are most urgent.

Poland, Germany, Nepal lead immigration; Mexico→USA and India→UAE are the critical bilateral corridors

Time Series Analysis & Forecasting

25-Year Migration Trends

Longitudinal analysis of migration rates from 2000–2025 — showing diverging trends between countries and the long-term policy impacts on population flows.

US decreasing (−0.105/year) while Germany increasing (+0.010/year) — policy is shaping these trajectories

Multi-Model Forecasting Comparison

ARIMA, Prophet, and Linear models tested head-to-head with error metrics — identifying which approach gives the most reliable 5-year planning forecast.

Analytical Insights

  • Stable destinations: US, Germany, and Saudi Arabia are the three consistent top-destination countries across the full 25-year period — infrastructure investment there has a long planning horizon
  • Dominant corridors: India, Mexico, and Russia are the dominant source countries — bilateral agreements targeting these three would address a disproportionate share of global migration policy challenges
  • Model superiority: ARIMA outperforms Prophet and Linear models by 65% — for policy planning, model selection is not academic, it changes the quality of the forecast
  • Robust clustering: 5–6 country clusters validated by four independent metrics — policymakers can be confident the groupings are statistically sound

Key Insights & Strategic Recommendations

Migration data is only useful if it changes decisions. Here is what this analysis means for each stakeholder group — and what specific actions the data supports.

Critical Findings

The Dominant Destinations Are Stable

The United States, Germany, and Saudi Arabia have been the top migrant destinations consistently across the full 25-year dataset. This stability means infrastructure, integration, and policy investments in these three countries have a long planning horizon — they will not stop being major destination countries soon.

Three Source Countries Drive the Majority of Flows

India, Mexico, and Russia show sustained high emigration throughout the period. The Mexico→USA and India→UAE corridors are the two most quantitatively significant bilateral flows in the dataset. Bilateral agreements targeting these specific corridors would address a disproportionately large share of global migration policy challenges.

Wealth Predicts Migration — But Policy Shapes It

The 0.62 correlation between national wealth and net immigration confirms that economic pull factors are real and persistent. But the diverging US (decreasing) and Germany (increasing) trend lines over 25 years show that policy choices layer on top of the economic baseline — migration is manageable, not simply determined by wealth alone.

Recommendations

International NGOs

Prioritise resource allocation and language/cultural integration programmes in Germany and Saudi Arabia — the two non-English destination countries receiving the largest flows from top source nations. These are where integration investment has the greatest return on humanitarian impact.

Policy Makers

Develop targeted bilateral agreements with India and Mexico first — these two source countries account for a disproportionate share of the major migration corridors. Skills-matching frameworks built around the specific industries where these migrants cluster would make agreements more durable.

Urban Planners

Anticipate infrastructure and housing needs in the major destination cities within the US and Germany. The 25-year trend data makes clear these inflows are structural, not temporary — planning for them now is cheaper than reacting to them later.

Development Organisations

Use the 5-year ARIMA forecasts to set proactive planning targets rather than reactive annual reviews. The model's 0.25 MAE gives a reliable enough signal to build multi-year infrastructure and service delivery plans around predicted population movements.
